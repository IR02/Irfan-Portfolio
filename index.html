Quantum Heist

A creative and engaging quantum hackathon project that transforms the weighted Max-Cut problem into a realistic bank robbery scenario. The bank's vault network is modeled as a 5-node graph where nodes represent vaults and edges represent guarded pathways or critical alarm triggers.

By assigning an extremely high weight of 10,000 to the alarm edge between nodes 3 and 4, we ensure QAOA always includes this edge in the cut, forcing the decoy team to pass through and silently disable the main alarm system. High weights on other edges (such as 1-2) mark the locations of elite security guards that must be neutralized first, allowing the main robbery crew to reach the vaults undetected.

Running QAOA produces a clear sampling histogram with four dominant bitstrings (10010, 01101, 10101, 01010). Although the global maximum cut creates two balanced partitions, we discovered that the near-optimal solutions with exactly two 1s naturally divide the bank into three disconnected regions. This enables three simultaneous strike teams to enter and clear multiple vaults in parallel, significantly reducing total heist time. Our detailed post-processing, strategic insight, and clear visualizations of both the weighted graph and final partition elevate a standard QAOA implementation into a compelling, mission-optimized quantum-powered heist plan.

CFD Analysis of Turbulent Flow over a Backward-Facing Step

A high-fidelity 2D CFD investigation of incompressible turbulent flow over a classic backward-facing step benchmark (Driver & Seegmiller, 1985) was performed using ANSYS Fluent. The computational domain was carefully designed with an optimized inlet length of 0.76 m to develop a fully turbulent boundary layer matching the experimental thickness of 19 mm at 4 step heights upstream of the expansion.

A structured mesh with 20 inflation layers (first cell height ensuring y⁺ < 1 everywhere), growth rate 1.36, and localized face sizing delivered excellent near-wall resolution and mesh quality (average aspect ratio 1.11).

The k-ω Shear Stress Transport (SST) model with Low-Reynolds correction and production limiter was selected for its proven superiority in predicting separation and reattachment under adverse pressure gradients. A pressure-based coupled solver with second-order discretization for all equations ensured fast, stable convergence (82 iterations to residuals < 10⁻⁵, further stabilized to 500 iterations). Hybrid initialization and monitoring of turbulent kinetic energy at key wall locations confirmed both numerical and physical convergence.

Quantitative validation against experimental skin-friction coefficient data showed outstanding agreement: predicted reattachment length at x/H = 6.18 versus experimental 6.28 (error only 1.59%). Velocity contours, vectors, pressure fields, and turbulent kinetic energy distributions accurately captured the separation bubble, clockwise recirculation vortex, shear-layer growth, and downstream recovery, demonstrating that the SST model, combined with wall-resolved meshing and high-order numerics, reliably reproduces this canonical separated-flow benchmark.

A Comparative Vibrational Analysis on Three Different Steam Turbine Blades

Modal analysis was performed on three steam turbine blades using tap tests at ten points arranged in a 5×2 grid. Frequency response functions were computed from five averaged impacts per point.

The first five natural frequencies were extracted as follows: Blade 1 (120.25–644.5 Hz), Blade 2 (116.75–645.75 Hz), and Blade 3 (112.00–650.75 Hz), confirming Blade 1 was stiffest and Blade 3 most flexible. Blade 2 was intermediate, with a first-mode frequency ~7% below Blade 1. Modes were consistent in shape, but Blade 2 and 3 showed slightly lower frequency integrity.


Damping ratios, determined via the half-power method, ranged from 0.025 to 0.0021 for Blade 1 and showed similar trends in Blades 2 and 3. Blade 2 exhibited the highest damping and greater root motion, indicating potential wear, softer boundary conditions, or friction-related energy dissipation. MAC values exceeded 0.9 in modes 1–3, confirming good mode shape agreement. No processing errors were evident; observed variations are attributed to physical differences in structure, support, or operational wear.


Low damping (ζ ~0.002–0.003) implies high Q-factors (~20–40), enabling resonance to amplify vibratory strain by up to 40×. This poses a significant fatigue risk, as a 2× increase in stress amplitude can reduce life by 10×. Detuning from resonance particularly near Blade 3's first mode at 112 Hz is essential. These results inform blade selection, quality control (e.g. MAC < 0.9 or >5% frequency deviation), finite element model validation, and structural monitoring for recommissioning.
